# Chapter 15: Avoiding Bubbles

> "This time it's different."

> "Bull markets are born on pessimism, grown on skepticism, mature on optimism, and die on euphoria."

Both quotes are associated with Sir John Templeton, and together they capture an important lesson for every investor: bubbles do happen from time to time, and they can be extremely dangerous. While it is impossible to predict every bubble perfectly, investors can often avoid the worst damage if they recognize some of the warning signs.

## What Is a Bubble?

A bubble happens when the price of an asset rises far above its reasonable underlying value, mainly because people expect they can sell it later at an even higher price. In the early stages, there may be a real and exciting story behind the asset:
- A new technology
- A new business model
- Easy access to credit
- A belief that the world has permanently changed

Sometimes the story contains some truth. The internet really did change the world. Artificial intelligence may also reshape many industries. But even when the story is true, the price paid can still become absurd.

That is the heart of a bubble: **a good story combined with unrealistic expectations and excessive prices**.

## Why Bubbles Are So Dangerous

Bubbles are dangerous because they encourage investors to ignore valuation, risk, and common sense.

When prices keep rising:
- Greed starts to overpower discipline
- Investors fear being left behind
- Speculation gets mistaken for skill
- Losses from bad decisions are temporarily hidden by rising prices

The biggest problem is that bubbles often end with **large permanent capital losses**. If you buy at a wildly inflated price, even a good asset may produce poor long-term returns. A wonderful business can still be a terrible investment if you pay too much for it.

## The Most Dangerous Phrase: "This Time It's Different"

Every bubble comes with a narrative explaining why old valuation methods no longer matter.

People say things like:
- "Profits do not matter yet"
- "This industry will grow forever"
- "Traditional metrics are obsolete"
- "If you wait for a reasonable price, you will miss the opportunity"

Sometimes there really is a structural shift in the economy. But human psychology does not change much. Fear and greed repeat across generations. When investors start claiming that normal rules no longer apply, caution is usually warranted.

## Common Warning Signs of a Bubble

No single sign proves that a bubble exists, but several signs appearing together should make you more careful.

### 1. Prices Rise Much Faster Than Fundamentals

If stock prices are rising much faster than revenue, earnings, cash flow, or asset values, that is a warning sign.

For example:
- A company doubles in price, but profits barely grow
- An entire sector surges even though most firms remain unprofitable
- Valuation multiples expand dramatically without clear justification

When price moves far ahead of business reality, speculation may be taking over.

### 2. Valuation Stops Mattering

In normal markets, investors care about valuation metrics such as:
- Price-to-earnings ratio (P/E)
- Price-to-sales ratio (P/S)
- Free cash flow
- Dividend yield
- Return on capital

In a bubble, these measures are often dismissed as irrelevant. Investors focus only on momentum, narratives, or vague future potential.

This is dangerous because investing ultimately depends on the cash an asset can generate for its owners.

### 3. Everyone Starts Talking About It

When an asset class dominates conversations everywhere, speculation may be spreading.

Examples include:
- Friends with no investing background suddenly offering stock tips
- Social media filled with stories of easy money
- Taxi drivers, colleagues, and relatives discussing the same "can’t lose" opportunity
- News headlines celebrating dramatic gains every day

Popular enthusiasm alone does not prove a bubble, but widespread excitement often appears near the later stages.

### 4. Easy Money and Easy Credit Fuel the Boom

Bubbles often grow when money is cheap and borrowing is easy.

If people can easily borrow to buy assets, prices may rise beyond sustainable levels. Leverage makes gains look bigger on the way up, but it also makes the collapse more severe on the way down.

### 5. New Investors Enter Mainly for Quick Gains

Healthy investing is about owning productive assets for long-term returns. Bubble behavior is different. People buy mainly because they expect the price to rise quickly.

You may hear statements like:
- "I do not really understand it, but it keeps going up"
- "I will just hold it for a few weeks"
- "Someone else will buy it from me at a higher price"

That is speculation, not investment.

### 6. Extreme Confidence Replaces Humility

Late in a bubble, people stop speaking cautiously. They become certain.

Common attitudes include:
- "This asset cannot fall much"
- "The government will never allow it to collapse"
- "Anyone who is cautious just does not get it"
- "The smart move is to buy every dip"

Confidence is useful in investing, but certainty is dangerous. Markets are always capable of surprising us.

## Historical Examples

### The Dot-Com Bubble

In the late 1990s, internet companies attracted enormous excitement. The internet was indeed revolutionary, but many companies had weak business models, little revenue, and no profits. Investors bought anyway because they believed future growth would justify any price.

Some companies eventually became hugely successful. But many disappeared completely, and even strong businesses were terrible investments if bought at peak prices.

This is an important lesson: **a great innovation does not guarantee a great investment at any price**.

### More Recent Manias

Different periods have had different bubble candidates:
- Housing booms
- Meme stocks
- Cryptocurrency surges
- AI-related speculation

Some of these areas may contain real long-term value. But during speculative phases, investors often overpay because enthusiasm outruns business reality.

## An AI Bubble Looming?

Artificial intelligence may become one of the most important technologies of our time. It could improve productivity, transform industries, and create enormous economic value. In that sense, excitement about AI is not automatically irrational.

However, a powerful technology can still become the center of a speculative bubble if investors begin paying unrealistic prices.

Warning signs to watch for include:
- Companies adding "AI" to their story without clear business results
- Stock prices rising much faster than revenue, profits, or cash flow
- Investors assuming every AI-related company will become a winner
- Valuations that already price in years of perfect execution
- Market participants dismissing all concerns by saying AI changes everything

This does not mean AI is fake or unimportant. The internet was real, but the dot-com bubble was real too. Both statements can be true at the same time.

That is why disciplined investors should separate **technology** from **valuation**:
- A technology can be revolutionary
- A company can be excellent
- But the stock can still be overpriced

The right question is not, "Is AI important?" The better question is, "Am I paying a reasonable price for the opportunities and risks involved?"

If AI continues to attract euphoric speculation, some AI-related stocks may eventually suffer large declines even if the technology itself succeeds over the long term.

### Warning Signs We May Already Be Seeing

There are also a few warning signs that some investors believe are already visible today.

At the top of the AI ecosystem, companies such as OpenAI and Anthropic are widely seen as the key demand creators. They are driving spending across cloud infrastructure, chips, and semiconductor equipment. Yet these companies are also believed to be losing very large sums of money, potentially billions of dollars per quarter. Because they are private companies, outside investors do not get the same level of transparency they would get from a public company’s quarterly filings.

This matters because many downstream businesses may be benefiting from spending that is not yet economically self-sustaining. If the major AI model companies are raising vast amounts of external capital and then spending aggressively on compute, that money flows through the entire chain:

- Private capital funds OpenAI and Anthropic
- OpenAI and Anthropic spend heavily on cloud providers
- Cloud providers buy more AI chips and infrastructure
- Chipmakers and semiconductor firms report stronger revenue and earnings

Companies that may benefit from this chain include cloud providers, AI chip companies such as Nvidia, and semiconductor-related firms such as TSMC, ASML, and Broadcom. The risk is that some of these reported earnings may look stronger than they would under a fully self-funding, economically mature demand environment.

In other words, investors should ask whether current demand is truly durable, or whether some of it is being temporarily amplified by abundant private capital.

Other warning signs include:

- Extremely high valuations, including reports of valuations around 30 times sales for leading private AI firms(OpenAI/Anthropic)
- Heavy subsidization of AI usage, where subscription pricing may be far below the underlying cost of the compute being consumed. For example, some reports and commentators claim that a roughly $200 monthly subscription can deliver thousands of dollars' worth of model usage, suggesting demand may be supported by unsustainably generous pricing rather than normal economics
- Circular deals, where companies invest in one another or structure arrangements that make demand appear stronger than it really is
- The use of special purpose vehicles (SPVs) or off-balance-sheet funding structures to raise money and sustain spending

These signs do not prove fraud or guarantee a collapse. But they should make investors more careful. Simply observing "strong earnings" from a company like Nvidia without asking where the end demand ultimately comes from may be too narrow a way to analyze the risk.

If free-flowing private capital eventually slows, the consequences could move through the same chain in reverse:

- AI labs reduce spending
- Cloud demand growth slows
- Orders for chips and semiconductor equipment weaken
- Market expectations reset lower

That is how investors can suffer permanent capital losses. A company may remain excellent, but if its stock was priced for uninterrupted explosive growth, even a modest slowdown can cause a sharp drop in valuation.

Bulls will argue that AI is clearly the future, and they may be right in a broad technological sense. They may also argue that companies such as OpenAI and Anthropic will eventually become highly profitable. That may also happen.

But even if the long-term story is true, investors still need to remember a basic principle: **future potential does not protect you from overpaying today**. Many semiconductor and AI-related companies are already valued as if strong future growth is virtually guaranteed. When expectations are that high, any disappointment can translate directly into painful capital losses.

## How to Avoid Getting Caught in a Bubble

### Focus on Valuation

Always ask:
- What am I paying?
- What cash flows or earnings support this price?
- What assumptions must come true for this investment to work?

If the answer depends on extreme optimism, be careful.

### Beware the DCF Trap

A discounted cash flow (DCF) model can be a useful valuation tool, but it can also create a false sense of precision. One of the most important inputs in a DCF is the growth projection. If that growth assumption is too optimistic, the model can make an expensive stock appear "undervalued."

This is one of the classic traps during speculative periods. Investors build spreadsheets with aggressive revenue growth, expanding margins, and long runways, then conclude that the current price is justified. But if those assumptions are even slightly too optimistic, the valuation can fall apart very quickly.

In simple terms, a DCF is only as reliable as its assumptions. If you assume years of exceptional growth, strong profitability, and minimal setbacks, you may simply be converting optimism into a neat-looking valuation model.

The same caution applies to **forward P/E ratios**. A stock can appear cheap on a forward P/E basis if analysts are assuming very strong earnings growth next year. But if those earnings fail to arrive, the "cheap" valuation was an illusion.

Forward P/E should therefore be taken with a grain of salt, especially when the expected growth rate is above 10% and the business is in a highly uncertain or competitive environment. The higher the assumed growth, the more careful you should be.

When using valuation models, it is wise to ask:
- What happens if growth is slower than expected?
- What happens if margins do not improve?
- What happens if demand normalizes?
- Am I valuing the business, or merely extrapolating recent excitement?

Good investing requires humility. If a valuation only works under very optimistic assumptions, that is often a warning sign rather than a margin of safety.

### Stay Within Your Circle of Competence

If you do not understand how a business makes money, what risks it faces, and what its reasonable value might be, you should be cautious. Bubble areas often attract people who buy first and try to understand later.

### Be Willing to Miss Out

One of the hardest parts of bubble avoidance is psychological. You may watch others make fast gains while you stay disciplined. That can feel frustrating.

But successful investing is not about participating in every craze. It is about protecting capital and compounding sensibly over long periods.

Missing part of a speculative boom is far less harmful than being caught in the collapse.

### Use Position Sizing and Diversification

If you decide to invest in a fast-growing or uncertain area, avoid letting it become too large a part of your portfolio. Diversification cannot eliminate losses, but it can prevent one bad mania from destroying your financial future.

### Remember That Government Bonds Can Be an Option

You do not need to be fully invested in every environment. Sometimes the wise choice is patience and safety rather than chasing overpriced assets.

Sir John Templeton famously pointed out that during the dot-com bubble, investors could buy government bonds yielding around 6%. That is a useful reminder that when speculative assets are priced for perfection, a safer asset with a reasonable yield can be an attractive alternative.

In other words, you do not always need to reach for excitement. When markets become euphoric, it can make sense to hold assets such as short-duration government bonds and wait for better opportunities.

## Templeton's Market Cycle Insight

Templeton's quote about bull markets is useful because it reminds us that market psychology evolves:

- **Born on pessimism**: Few people want to invest
- **Grown on skepticism**: Investors doubt the rally
- **Mature on optimism**: Confidence becomes widespread
- **Die on euphoria**: People become convinced prices can only rise

Euphoria is the most dangerous stage. It is when risk appears lowest, even though actual risk may be highest.

## Final Thought

Bubbles are a recurring feature of financial markets because human emotions are recurring features of human nature. You will probably live through several speculative manias as an investor.

You do not need to identify every bubble perfectly. You only need enough discipline to notice the warning signs, avoid overpaying, and refuse to join a crowd that has lost touch with reality.

In investing, survival matters. Avoiding one catastrophic bubble can do more for your long-term wealth than chasing the excitement of the moment.
